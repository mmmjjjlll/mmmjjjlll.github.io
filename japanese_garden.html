Introduction

Japanese dry landscape (karesansui) gardens exhibit sophisticated visual designs with minimal compositions and engender a calm, contemplative atmosphere. Here we use perceptual models to study Japanese garden design with the aim of discovering guidelines for the creation of calm visual spaces.

Overview

Models of scene understanding reveal how boudary contour, junction, and surface texture elements are combined by the visual system into the patterns which we perceive when looking at a scene. Gestalt laws of proximity, similarity, smoothness, and enclosedness provide an intuitive set of cues to understand this process. Analogously in karesansui gardens, visual heuristics are used to guide selection and control of visual pattern elements to express calmness and avoid conflicting, confusing scenes. Several design heuristics are at work in the figure below, for example, the fractal-like repetition of a triangular pattern, giving this garden a harmonious, natural appearance.

In karesansui gardens, considerable emphasis is placed on the beauty of "empty" space. A famous example is the dry landscape garden at Ryoanji temple (see figure below) which consists of a sparse arrangement of five clusters of 15 rocks and some moss surrounded by an expanse of raked gravel. Medial axis transform analysis, studied in models of perception and in computer vision, is useful for visualizing the structure of these empty spaces. The medial axis transform of the empty space in the Ryoanji garden reveals a non-accidental, simple, self-similar structure, which may explain the strong visual appeal of this garden. Consideration of visual Gestalt cues, multi-scale patterns and the structure of empty space deepends insight into the perceptual basis of traditional garden aesthetic qualities of asymmetry, simplicity, naturalness, and tranquility. We are investigating general guidelines for calming visual environments. This research may be use to those who wish to create calm visual spaces, virtual or real.
